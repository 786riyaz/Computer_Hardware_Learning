# Computer Ports: Data and Video Port Details (Including USB 3 and SATA Versions)

## Data Ports (Sorted by Maximum Theoretical Data Transfer Speed, Descending)

1. Thunderbolt 4
   - Speed: 40 Gbps (5 GB/s)
   - Description: Supports data, video, and power over USB-C connectors. Backward compatible with Thunderbolt 3 and USB standards.
   - Use: High-speed peripherals, external storage, displays.

2. USB4 (Version 2.0)
   - Speed: 40 Gbps (5 GB/s), up to 80 Gbps (10 GB/s) in specific configurations
   - Description: Built on Thunderbolt 3/4 architecture, uses USB-C connectors, supports data, power delivery, and video. Backward compatible with USB 3.2 and earlier.
   - Use: External drives, docking stations, displays.

3. NVMe (PCIe 5.0 x4)
   - Speed: ~32 Gbps (~4 GB/s) for PCIe 5.0 x4; ~16 Gbps (~2 GB/s) for PCIe 4.0 x4
   - Description: Protocol for SSDs, typically used over PCIe lanes (often via M.2 slots). Speed depends on PCIe version and lane count.
   - Use: Internal high-speed storage (SSDs).

4. M.2 (PCIe 5.0 x4)
   - Speed: ~32 Gbps (~4 GB/s) for PCIe 5.0 x4; ~16 Gbps (~2 GB/s) for PCIe 4.0 x4
   - Description: Physical slot supporting NVMe (PCIe) or SATA protocols. Speed depends on protocol and PCIe version. Fits M.2 modules (e.g., SSDs, Wi-Fi cards) with keying (B-key, M-key, B+M-key) and sizes (e.g., 2280).
   - Use: SSDs, Wi-Fi cards, expansion cards.
   - Note: Internal slot on motherboards, not an external port. Requires motherboard compatibility for protocol (PCIe/SATA) and size.

5. SATA 3.2 (SATA Express)
   - Speed: Up to 16 Gbps (2 GB/s) using PCIe 3.0 x2 lanes; 6 Gbps (750 MB/s) for SATA mode
   - Description: Hybrid standard combining SATA and PCIe for faster SSDs. Rarely adopted due to NVMe and M.2 dominance.
   - Use: Limited to specific motherboards/devices; largely obsolete.

6. USB 3.2 Gen 2x2 (SuperSpeed USB 20Gbps)
   - Speed: 20 Gbps (2.5 GB/s)
   - Description: Uses dual-lane USB-C connectors for high-speed data transfer. Part of USB 3.2 standard, introduced in 2017. Backward compatible with USB 3.0/3.1 and USB 2.0.
   - Use: External SSDs, high-performance peripherals.
   - Version Note: No USB 3.1 equivalent; requires compatible devices/cables for full speed.

7. USB 3.2 Gen 2 (SuperSpeed USB 10Gbps)
   - Speed: 10 Gbps (1.25 GB/s)
   - Description: Uses USB-C or USB-A connectors, widely used for fast external devices. Originally USB 3.1 Gen 2 (2013), rebranded as USB 3.2 Gen 2. Backward compatible with USB 3.0 and USB 2.0.
   - Use: External drives, peripherals.
   - Version Note: Doubled USB 3.0 speed, supports enhanced power delivery (up to 100W with USB PD).

8. SATA III (SATA 3.0)
   - Speed: 6 Gbps (750 MB/s)
   - Description: Mainstream standard for internal HDDs and SSDs. Practical speed ~600 MB/s due to protocol overhead. Supports NCQ and power management.
   - Use: Internal storage (HDDs, SSDs).
   - Note: M.2 slots supporting SATA SSDs operate at this speed.

9. USB 3.2 Gen 1 (SuperSpeed USB 5Gbps)
   - Speed: 5 Gbps (625 MB/s)
   - Description: First USB 3.0 generation (2008), rebranded as USB 3.2 Gen 1. Uses USB-A, USB-B, or USB-C connectors. Backward compatible with USB 2.0.
   - Use: External drives, peripherals.
   - Version Note: Introduced SuperSpeed USB, 10x faster than USB 2.0.

10. SATA II (SATA 2.0)
    - Speed: 3 Gbps (300 MB/s)
    - Description: Doubled SATA I speed, practical throughput ~240 MB/s. Improved NCQ and staggered spin-up.
    - Use: Mid-2000s HDDs and early SSDs, supported in legacy systems.

11. Ethernet (10GbE)
    - Speed: 10 Gbps (1.25 GB/s)
    - Description: High-speed networking port for LAN connections.
    - Use: Network connectivity, NAS.

12. Ethernet (1GbE)
    - Speed: 1 Gbps (125 MB/s)
    - Description: Standard Gigabit Ethernet, common in most computers.
    - Use: Network connectivity.

13. SATA I (SATA 1.0)
    - Speed: 1.5 Gbps (150 MB/s)
    - Description: First SATA standard, practical throughput ~120 MB/s. Introduced hot-swapping and NCQ.
    - Use: Early SATA HDDs and SSDs, now obsolete.

14. USB 2.0
    - Speed: 480 Mbps (60 MB/s)
    - Description: Older USB standard, used for low-bandwidth devices. Significantly slower than USB 3.2, M.2 (NVMe), or SATA III.
    - Use: Keyboards, mice, older peripherals.

## Video Ports and Supported Resolutions

1. VGA (Video Graphics Array)
   - Maximum Resolution: 1920x1200 @ 60Hz (WUXGA)
   - Details: Analog signal, no fixed bandwidth. Practical max often 1920x1080 or 1600x1200 @ 60Hz. Higher resolutions (e.g., 2048x1536 @ 60Hz) possible with high-quality cables but rare.
   - Use Case: Legacy monitors, projectors.
   - Note: No general data transfer capability, unlike USB or M.2.

2. HDMI (High-Definition Multimedia Interface)
   - HDMI 1.4:
     - Maximum Resolution: 4K (4096x2160) @ 30Hz or 1080p @ 120Hz
     - Details: 10.2 Gbps bandwidth, supports 3D.
   - HDMI 2.0:
     - Maximum Resolution: 4K (4096x2160) @ 60Hz
     - Details: 18 Gbps bandwidth, supports HDR.
   - HDMI 2.1:
     - Maximum Resolution: 8K (7680x4320) @ 60Hz or 4K @ 120Hz (with Dynamic HDR)
     - Details: 48 Gbps bandwidth, supports Variable Refresh Rate (VRR), Auto Low Latency Mode (ALLM), and 10K (10240x4320) @ lower rates with Display Stream Compression (DSC).
   - Use Case: TVs, monitors, gaming consoles, home theater systems.

3. DisplayPort (DP)
   - DisplayPort 1.2:
     - Maximum Resolution: 4K (3840x2160) @ 60Hz or 2560x1600 @ 120Hz
     - Details: 17.28 Gbps bandwidth, supports multi-monitor setups via daisy-chaining.
   - DisplayPort 1.4:
     - Maximum Resolution: 8K (7680x4320) @ 30Hz or 4K @ 120Hz (with HDR)
     - Details: 32.4 Gbps bandwidth, supports DSC for 8K @ 60Hz or 4K @ 240Hz.
   - DisplayPort 2.0:
     - Maximum Resolution: 16K (15360x8460) @ 60Hz with DSC, 8K @ 120Hz without DSC, or 4K @ 240Hz
     - Details: 80 Gbps bandwidth, designed for high-resolution displays, supports adaptive sync and HDR.
   - Use Case: High-end monitors, multi-monitor setups, VR, professional displays.

## Additional Video Port Notes
- Bandwidth Context: HDMI and DisplayPort bandwidths (e.g., 48 Gbps for HDMI 2.1) are for video/audio, not general data transfer, so they are listed separately from data ports like USB, M.2, or SATA.
- Cables: High resolutions require specific cables (e.g., Ultra High Speed HDMI for HDMI 2.1, DP80 for DP 2.0).
- Compression: DisplayPort’s DSC and HDMI 2.1’s FRL enable higher resolutions/refresh rates without visible quality loss.
- Hardware: GPU, monitor, and port version must all support the desired resolution/refresh rate.

## USB 3 Versioning System
- USB 3.0 (2008):
  - Rebranded as USB 3.2 Gen 1 (5 Gbps, SuperSpeed USB).
  - Introduced 10x speed over USB 2.0, using USB-A, USB-B, or USB-C connectors.
- USB 3.1 Gen 2 (2013):
  - Rebranded as USB 3.2 Gen 2 (10 Gbps, SuperSpeed USB 10Gbps).
  - Doubled USB 3.0 speed, typically uses USB-C, supports up to 100W power delivery.
- USB 3.2 Gen 2x2 (2017):
  - 20 Gbps (SuperSpeed USB 20Gbps), uses dual-lane USB-C connectors.
  - No USB 3.1 equivalent, less common due to USB4/Thunderbolt adoption.
- Naming: USB-IF rebranded USB 3 versions in 2019 for clarity, but older terms (USB 3.0, 3.1) remain in use.
- Backward Compatibility: All USB 3 versions support USB 2.0 and USB 1.x, but speeds are limited to the slowest component.

## Specific Notes on M.2, USB 2.0, SATA, and USB 3
- M.2 Placement:
  - With NVMe (PCIe 5.0 x4), M.2 ranks 4th at ~32 Gbps (~4 GB/s), tied with NVMe.
  - With SATA, M.2 ranks 8th at 6 Gbps (750 MB/s), tied with SATA III.
  - M.2 is an internal slot on the motherboard, not an external port. Supports NVMe (PCIe) or SATA protocols, with speed depending on motherboard’s PCIe version (e.g., 5.0, 4.0) and lane count (typically x4).
- USB 2.0 Placement:
  - Ranks 14th at 480 Mbps (60 MB/s), near the bottom due to low speed compared to USB 3.2, M.2 (NVMe), or SATA III.
  - Used for low-bandwidth peripherals, not suitable for high-speed data transfer or video output.
- SATA Versioning:
  - SATA I (1.5 Gbps, 13th place): First standard (2003), now obsolete.
  - SATA II (3 Gbps, 10th place): Improved speed/features, used in mid-2000s, legacy support.
  - SATA III (6 Gbps, 8th place): Mainstream standard as of 2025, used for HDDs/SSDs.
  - SATA 3.2 (up to 16 Gbps, 5th place): Hybrid SATA/PCIe standard, rarely used.
  - Backward Compatibility: All SATA versions are compatible, but speeds are limited to the slowest component.
- USB 3 Versioning:
  - USB 3.2 Gen 1 (5 Gbps, 9th place): Originally USB 3.0, for external drives/peripherals.
  - USB 3.2 Gen 2 (10 Gbps, 7th place): Originally USB 3.1 Gen 2, for faster external storage.
  - USB 3.2 Gen 2x2 (20 Gbps, 6th place): Dual-lane USB-C, for high-performance SSDs.
  - Comparison: USB 3.2 Gen 2x2 is 40x faster than USB 2.0, but 2x slower than USB4/Thunderbolt 4.

## Comparisons
1. USB 3 Versions:
   - Speed: Gen 1 (5 Gbps) is 10x USB 2.0; Gen 2 (10 Gbps) is 2x Gen 1; Gen 2x2 (20 Gbps) is 2x Gen 2.
   - Use Cases: Gen 1 for basic external drives; Gen 2 for modern SSDs/docks; Gen 2x2 for professional SSDs (limited adoption).
   - Connectors: Gen 1 supports USB-A/B/C; Gen 2 prefers USB-C; Gen 2x2 requires USB-C.
   - Practical: Actual speeds ~70–80% of theoretical (e.g., ~4 Gbps for Gen 1, ~8 Gbps for Gen 2).

2. USB 3 vs. SATA III:
   - Speed: SATA III (6 Gbps, ~600 MB/s) is faster than USB 3.2 Gen 1 (5 Gbps, ~400–500 MB/s) but slower than Gen 2 (10 Gbps, ~800–1000 MB/s) or Gen 2x2 (20 Gbps).
   - Use: SATA III for internal HDDs/SSDs; USB 3 for external storage/peripherals.
   - Latency: SATA III has lower latency internally; USB 3 adds slight overhead externally.
   - Note: USB 3.2 Gen 2 often outperforms SATA III in external SSD enclosures.

3. USB 3 vs. M.2 (NVMe):
   - Speed: M.2 (NVMe PCIe 5.0 x4, ~32 Gbps) is up to 6x faster than USB 3.2 Gen 2x2 (20 Gbps).
   - Use: M.2 (NVMe) for internal high-performance SSDs; USB 3 for external storage.
   - Form Factor: M.2 is internal (motherboard slot); USB 3 is external (plug-and-play).
   - Note: NVMe M.2 in USB 3.2 Gen 2 enclosure is limited to ~10 Gbps.

4. USB 3 vs. USB 2.0:
   - Speed: USB 3.2 Gen 1 is ~10x USB 2.0; Gen 2 is 20x; Gen 2x2 is 40x.
   - Use: USB 2.0 for keyboards/mice; USB 3 for external drives.
   - Compatibility: USB 3 ports support USB 2.0 devices at 480 Mbps.

5. SATA vs. M.2:
   - Speed: SATA III (6 Gbps) matches M.2 (SATA) but is slower than M.2 (NVMe, up to 32 Gbps).
   - Use: SATA III for budget HDDs/SSDs; M.2 (NVMe) for high-performance SSDs.
   - Form Factor: SATA uses cables; M.2 is slot-based, compact.

6. USB 3 vs. Thunderbolt 4/USB4:
   - Speed: Thunderbolt 4/USB4 (40 Gbps) are 2x faster than USB 3.2 Gen 2x2 (20 Gbps).
   - Features: Thunderbolt 4/USB4 support video and daisy-chaining; USB 3.2 is data-focused.
   - Cost: USB 3.2 devices/cables are cheaper and more common.

## General Notes
- Practical Speeds: Theoretical maximums (e.g., 32 Gbps for M.2 PCIe 5.0, 6 Gbps for SATA III) are reduced by protocol overhead, device limitations, or cable quality.
- Obsolete Ports: Ports like FireWire or eSATA are excluded as they are largely obsolete.
